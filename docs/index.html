Why does a breast ultrasound robot lose the tumor when you lie on your side? Posture shift deforms tissue enough to break the scan path. This paper fits patient-specific elastic models from RGB-D cameras alone and cuts localization error by 62%… http://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/11599450

Tendons lie. At every direction reversal, hysteresis hides the true tip position, and you can't fit a sensor there. Hong & Hong show the proximal force plateau already carries the answer. https://doi.org/10.1109/lra.2026.3711853

The core idea: reverse an antagonistic tendon pair and proximal force flatlines before the distal load moves. That plateau width maps to hysteresis magnitude. No distal sensor, no added hardware. Just reading what's already there more carefully.
My question for anyone running cable-driven inspection arms or endoscopic tools: does the plateau shape stay consistent when sheath routing geometry changes mid-deployment? Bench results are clean. Real sheaths flex. Has anyone pushed this into a dynamic routing scenario?

Your orchard robot snaps a branch. MPC saw it coming. Real-time torque sensing + recursive least squares branch stiffness model + MPC replanning = contact forces suppressed, 1mm accuracy held, no joint overload. Clever abstraction. Still… http://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/11593107

The 'virtual contact body' is the part worth stealing. Instead of reacting to force as raw data, they model the branch as a stiffness element and fit it live with RLS. MPC then treats it as a constraint, not a disturbance. That's a cleaner problem formulation than most reactive planners use.
Caveat that matters: every result comes from a simulated harvesting scenario. Real canopies have wind load, variable fruit mass, and branches that snap rather than deform smoothly. Does RLS converge fast enough when contact dynamics change abruptly? That's the field validation nobody has run yet…
