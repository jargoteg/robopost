No sensors. No code. No motors. Leiden microrobots move purely through shape and environmental coupling. Behavior fabricated in, not programmed in. Clever physics. Clinical utility still unproven. https://robohub.org/developing-active-and-flexible-microrobots/

The core idea traces to Purcell's 1977 scallop theorem: at low Reynolds number, reciprocal motion gets you nowhere. You need asymmetric flexibility. That constraint is 50 years old. What's new is fabricating it reliably enough to call it a robot rather than a demonstration.
My honest take: this stays interesting if behavioral variety scales with fabrication control. If you can only get one or two reliable gaits, it's a physics result, not a robot. What's the minimum behavioral repertoire you'd need before calling it clinically useful?
