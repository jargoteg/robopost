One motor. No wheels, no legs. SAWbot propels itself with a continuous traveling wave along its body. Single actuator, principled design analysis, real pipe-inspection potential. The friction-surface problem is still the killer. https://doi.org/10.1109/lra.2026.3710339

The clever bit is producing a true continuous wave from one rotary input. Most undulatory robots discretize it into segments and lose efficiency. Zarrouk's group is building toward something cleaner mechanically. The analysis lets you tune amplitude, frequency, body geometry before you cut metal.
My question for anyone working in pipe inspection: how much does your target environment vary in friction? Because that single variable probably determines whether wave-drive is viable or stays a lab curiosity. Wet cast iron vs dry PVC vs concrete are three different robots.

Most surface water cleaning robots still need a human nearby. A new JFR review is honest about why: debris detection fails on real rivers, path planning breaks in current and wind, power budgets run out first. Hardware is ready. Autonomy is not. https://doi.org/10.1002/rob.70303

Offshore wind blade repair: a robot that holds grinding force steady on curved composite surfaces, tuned by a neural net predicting surface roughness in real time. Bench results beat PID and ADRC. The 80m field trial is the missing chapter. https://doi.org/10.1002/rob.70306

The clever bit is the gain scheduling: PSO-BP predicts material removal rate and surface roughness live, then feeds those predictions directly into the ADRC gains. Not a black box, not a lookup table. Closed-loop stability proven via Lyapunov under bounded disturbance. That's a real contribution.
The gap I'd push back on: every experiment is bench-scale. Salt water, 80m height, vibration from rotor imbalance, rope-access or drone mounting constraints, none of that is in the test conditions. Who's running an actual offshore field trial on blade grinding? That paper would be huge.

3 hours underwater, no GPS, no USBL, DVL unreliable. Position still holds. Marquardt & Kang detect current disturbances as statistical anomalies and correct the KF in real time, zero infrastructure. Validated offshore. https://doi.org/10.1002/rob.70279

The core move: online PELT flags when a current event hits the vehicle. That signal feeds directly into Kalman filter correction via recursive iteration. No pre-surveyed transponders, no dynamic model constraints. Works harder as currents get stronger, which is the opposite of most methods.
Caveat worth naming: PELT needs the current disturbance to be statistically distinguishable from vehicle dynamics. Highly turbulent or rapidly oscillating flow could blur that boundary. Anyone running AUV surveys in tidal channels or near-seafloor boundary layers, how often does that bite you?

Subsea grippers still can't match a diver's hand. A new JFR review explains why: sensing degrades, UVMS coupling compounds errors, and no standard benchmark exists to compare designs. First attempt at fixing that last part. https://doi.org/10.1002/rob.70277

The classification by force/form closure theory rather than actuator type is the right move. 'We use hydraulics' tells you almost nothing about grasping capability. The new framework actually maps to design decisions.
The UVMS coupling section is the most honest part: arm disturbs vehicle, vehicle correction disturbs arm, gripper tip error compounds. They flag it as an open problem. No lab has a clean answer. Worth asking: is this a controls problem or a platform architecture problem?

Egocentric teleoperation: you see what one robot sees. This paper fuses every robot's feed into a single top-down view. One operator, whole fleet, 84% faster on parallel tasks. Precision takes a hit. Worth that trade? https://doi.org/10.1126/scirobotics.adz7130

Rovers fail localization on poor textures, then waste compute trying anyway. This JFR paper flips it: detect matchable terrain first, trigger localization only then. Smarter constraint, not smarter matcher. https://doi.org/10.1002/rob.70298

The key move is separating the question 'can we localize here?' from 'should we try?' Most systems conflate them. Running a matchability classifier upstream is cheap. Running full DOM registration on bad terrain is expensive and still fails.
Caveat worth sitting with: the interest region detector needs training data tied to the target surface. First sol on a novel site, before you have any orbital texture priors, that classifier is flying blind. How robust is the fallback? Would love to see ablations on that.

Big hexapods hit a wall: full dynamics too slow, elastic deformation breaks your model. New JFR paper uses a Spring-Muscle abstraction to make real-time force control feasible on rough terrain transitions. Clever biomechanics shortcut. Prototype only so far. https://doi.org/10.1002/rob.70304

The core idea borrows from the Elastic Pendulum Hypothesis: model each leg as a spring-muscle unit instead of a full rigid chain. Cuts compute cost, keeps the physics honest enough for compliant foot-ground control. Two force distribution constraints handle the six-leg balancing act.
My caveat: 'border terrain' transitions are exactly where field robots fail in practice, and this hasn't been tested outside a principle prototype. No mud, no payload, no hydraulic backlash. How many of you have seen a simplified dynamics model survive first contact with a real site?
