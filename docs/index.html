Wheels or legs? Neither answer is enough. The real frontier is robots that blur the line between rolling, walking, and shape-changing on the fly. Here is the literature that built that idea.

Spherical tensegrity robots showed that a rolling shell can be steered on steep inclines by coordinating cable tensions, not by adding legs. This was an early proof that geometry itself could be a locomotion strategy, not just a support structure. arxiv.org/abs/1708.08150
OmniWheg made wheel-to-leg transformation cheap and omnidirectional. The separable omni-wheel plus 4-bar linkage is the kind of minimalist mechanism insight that unlocks a whole design family, and it shows up in later work directly. arxiv.org/abs/2203.02118
SWheg pushed minimalist actuator realization further, asking how few motors you actually need for a transformable wheel-leg robot. The modular family approach here is practical: swap modules, get a different gait, same controller skeleton. arxiv.org/abs/2210.15126
Rolling in the Deep brought online trajectory optimization to wheeled-legged hybrids, letting ANYmal-style platforms decide in real time when to roll and when to step. This is the control-theory anchor the hardware papers needed. arxiv.org/abs/1909.07193
Learning Robust Autonomous Navigation for Wheeled-Legged Robots scaled hybrid locomotion to full urban navigation stacks, connecting low-level gait switching to high-level path planning. Sim2real transfer is the hard part they quantify. arxiv.org/abs/2405.01792
ATRos frames the open problem cleanly: energy efficiency across wheeled and legged modes simultaneously. It is not enough to switch modes; you need a policy that knows when switching is worth the cost, and that metric has been underspecified until now. arxiv.org/abs/2510.09980

Boats steer poorly at low speed. Legs don't. QuadBoat puts four articulated limbs through the hull, propulsion and posture control in one, for the exact moment a thruster USV loses authority near a victim. https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13633v1

The control stack is MPC outer loop plus IK per-leg stroke. Outdoors pickup experiments work. But every test looks calm-water. Leg-surface coupling in chop or with an asymmetric victim load is the real unknown and the paper doesn't touch it.
The part I keep thinking about: same architecture could let it beach itself and keep going on land. That water-to-shore transition is where most rescue USVs just stop. Anyone working on amphibious legged platforms at this scale?

Wheels fail on rubble. 32 legs don't. Mochibot locomotes by asymmetric extension of legs on a rhombic triacontahedron: no steering hardware, true omnidirectionality, 31 backups if one leg dies. Elegant geometry. Still no… https://spectrum.ieee.org/32-legged-spherical-robot-moves-like-an-amoeba

The mechanism is genuinely clever. Retract legs in your direction of travel, extend the opposite side, center of mass shifts, sphere rolls. Any direction without a steering joint. The rhombic triacontahedron isn't arbitrary, it's the most uniform 32-point distribution on a sphere.
What kills me is this is IROS 2018 work. Eight years on, no published speed numbers, no dust/water sealing, no clear successor platform. The redundancy argument for disaster response or planetary use is real. So why hasn't anyone pushed it further? What's the blocker you'd bet on?

Why can't most underwater gliders fit through a door? Fixed wings. FoDeGlider folds and deflects its wings mid-swim, with a model that recomputes inertia and hydrodynamics at every pose. Gate traversal experiments back it up. https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13622v1

The dynamics modeling is the hard part here. Most morphing robot papers snapshot a few configurations and call it done. Treating wing pose as a continuous structural variable in a Fossen-form model is a cleaner approach, and the open parameter-ID dataset means others can actually build on it.
Caveat: no endurance numbers in the abstract. 'Long-endurance' and 'miniature morphing' are genuinely in tension, and the confined-space inspection pitch needs field data to land. What application would you want to see this tested in first?

Why can't robots cut hair safely yet? Sustained proximity to the human head, sharp tools, unpredictable user movement. A new JFR paper proposes a 3-layer safety framework to structure the problem. Useful vocabulary. The hard empirical validation is still ahead. https://doi.org/10.1002/rob.70305

The three layers: safe hardware design, control-level collision response, and interaction-level intent inference. Each must hold independently. Drawing on ISO/TS 15066 cobot force limits and healthcare fail-safe design is smart cross-domain borrowing.
Big open gaps the paper names but doesn't solve: real head-motion prediction under user anxiety, tool dynamics changing with wet hair, cross-cultural acceptance. Framework papers are only useful if someone runs the validation experiments next. Who's doing that work?

One motor. No wheels, no legs. SAWbot propels itself with a continuous traveling wave along its body. Single actuator, principled design analysis, real pipe-inspection potential. The friction-surface problem is still the killer. https://doi.org/10.1109/lra.2026.3710339

The clever bit is producing a true continuous wave from one rotary input. Most undulatory robots discretize it into segments and lose efficiency. Zarrouk's group is building toward something cleaner mechanically. The analysis lets you tune amplitude, frequency, body geometry before you cut metal.
My question for anyone working in pipe inspection: how much does your target environment vary in friction? Because that single variable probably determines whether wave-drive is viable or stays a lab curiosity. Wet cast iron vs dry PVC vs concrete are three different robots.

Most surface water cleaning robots still need a human nearby. A new JFR review is honest about why: debris detection fails on real rivers, path planning breaks in current and wind, power budgets run out first. Hardware is ready. Autonomy is not. https://doi.org/10.1002/rob.70303

The specific failure point is visual classification. Wet, tangled, mixed-material debris in a harbor is nothing like a test pond. Most detectors are tuned on clean conditions and degrade fast. This is a data problem as much as an algorithm problem.
Power budget is the second wall. Solar-assisted platforms extend range but the range-vs-payload tradeoff has no clean solution yet. If you're working on energy-aware path planning for aquatic systems, this review is a useful map of the unsolved problems. What's your read on where the field goes…
