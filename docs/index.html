This robot climbs inside your tomato plants. STEMbot navigates stems 7-33mm, builds a 3D plant map with onboard SLAM, and plans routes to leaf undersides where pests hide. Clever manifold A* planner, <1cm mapping error. Four lab plants so far. https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07873v1

The planner is the part worth studying. Manifold-constrained A* on the plant skeleton plus ray-tracing goal spec for occluded targets. That's a clean solution to a genuinely hard problem: how do you navigate to a surface you can't directly observe from your current position.
Honest question for anyone in precision ag: what's the actual bottleneck here? Is it the climbing mechanism, the canopy mapping, or just the sheer number of plants per hectare that makes per-plant inspection uneconomical regardless of robot cost?

One policy handles joint-angle, human-pose, AND end-effector references on a Unitree G1. No modality-specific retraining. 98.42% on unseen sim motions. The real-world contact story is what I want to see next. http://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/11576560

The architecture insight is real: joint angles and end-effector poses are different representational spaces and most controllers just pick one. Modality-specific encoders into a shared latent space is a clean solution. Whether it survives contact dynamics on hardware is the unresolved question.
Code is live at github.com/Renforce-Dynamics/MultiModalWBC and the open-access preprint is arxiv.org/abs/2606.04829. Curious if anyone has run this on hardware other than G1. How much does sim-to-real degrade on the manipulation side versus locomotion?
