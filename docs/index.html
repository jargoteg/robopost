Robots entered Fukushima Daiichi when humans could not. Several failed within hours. Wet floors, fogged cameras, tethers that snagged, comms that died through concrete. This paper is the most honest deployment post-mortem in nuclear robotics. https://doi.org/10.1002/rob.21439

Quince reached Building 2 stairwells, got irreplaceable sensor data, then got stranded by its own tether. That one failure mode, tether management under debris, still shows up in subsea and mining incidents today. Fifteen years and we have not fully fixed it.
The deeper problem was not hardware. It was zero interoperability between robot systems and TEPCO incident command, plus operators familiarizing themselves with robots during the crisis itself. Sound familiar to anyone working on inspection robot deployment right now?

Friction was killing vine robots. A triangular roller tip mount cuts tail tension and finally makes consistent sensor-carrying eversion work. Plus a reusable testbed to benchmark future designs. http://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/11592424

The benchmarking testbed might matter more than the mount itself. Measuring tail tension during eversion isolates the mount contribution cleanly. Now other groups can compare designs on the same terms. That kind of shared infrastructure is rare in soft robotics.

Everyone assumes you need hundreds of demos to train a manipulation policy. DynaMimicGen starts from one, uses DMPs to rebuild trajectories for dynamic scenes, and beats MimicGen by 232% on MugCleanup. Still sim-only, but the architecture matters. http://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/11568504

No depth sensor. No 3D tracker. Just pixel changes across two RGB frames driving a catch policy. Arm and hand as separate RL agents, cooperative rewards, sim-to-real confirmed. Genuinely clean idea. http://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/11563623

The heterogeneous multi-agent framing is the part worth stealing. Treating arm and hand as independent agents with role-specific rewards keeps credit assignment tractable. Monolithic policies on high-DoF systems are a headache. This sidesteps it.
My caveat: pixel-motion signals are fragile. Variable backgrounds, occlusion, off-axis throws. The lab is kind to this approach. Would love to see the failure modes documented. What conditions make the pixel representation break down first?
