This robot climbs inside your tomato plants. STEMbot navigates stems 7-33mm, builds a 3D plant map with onboard SLAM, and plans routes to leaf undersides where pests hide. Clever manifold A* planner, <1cm mapping error. Four lab plants so far. https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07873v1

The planner is the part worth studying. Manifold-constrained A* on the plant skeleton plus ray-tracing goal spec for occluded targets. That's a clean solution to a genuinely hard problem: how do you navigate to a surface you can't directly observe from your current position.
Honest question for anyone in precision ag: what's the actual bottleneck here? Is it the climbing mechanism, the canopy mapping, or just the sheer number of plants per hectare that makes per-plant inspection uneconomical regardless of robot cost?

One policy handles joint-angle, human-pose, AND end-effector references on a Unitree G1. No modality-specific retraining. 98.42% on unseen sim motions. The real-world contact story is what I want to see next. http://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/11576560

The architecture insight is real: joint angles and end-effector poses are different representational spaces and most controllers just pick one. Modality-specific encoders into a shared latent space is a clean solution. Whether it survives contact dynamics on hardware is the unresolved question.
Code is live at github.com/Renforce-Dynamics/MultiModalWBC and the open-access preprint is arxiv.org/abs/2606.04829. Curious if anyone has run this on hardware other than G1. How much does sim-to-real degrade on the manipulation side versus locomotion?

Why can't a humanoid just pick up a scalpel? Someone finally tested it in vivo. Teleoperation, standard laparoscopic tools, porcine tissue, benchmarked against da Vinci. Result: not ready, but now the gaps are quantified. https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07972v1

What does a strawberry-harvesting robot actually fail at in the field? Occlusion and lighting. Not the gripper. Not the arm. The perception stack that worked indoors. Full integration paper with real field results: https://doi.org/10.1002/rob.21889

The economics framing is the part I keep coming back to. A 3-hour field trial proves autonomy is possible. It says nothing about cost-per-punnet across a full season with weather, canopy variation, and fruit density changes. That is the number growers actually need.
Also worth saying: while everyone argues about humanoid form factors this week, this robot is a purpose-built low platform with a task-specific arm. The crop dictated the design. That is how field robotics actually works. What other specialty crops do you think are closest to commercial viability?

Robots entered Fukushima Daiichi when humans could not. Several failed within hours. Wet floors, fogged cameras, tethers that snagged, comms that died through concrete. This paper is the most honest deployment post-mortem in nuclear robotics. https://doi.org/10.1002/rob.21439

Quince reached Building 2 stairwells, got irreplaceable sensor data, then got stranded by its own tether. That one failure mode, tether management under debris, still shows up in subsea and mining incidents today. Fifteen years and we have not fully fixed it.
The deeper problem was not hardware. It was zero interoperability between robot systems and TEPCO incident command, plus operators familiarizing themselves with robots during the crisis itself. Sound familiar to anyone working on inspection robot deployment right now?
