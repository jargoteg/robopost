A proprioception-only RL controller trained in sim walks mud, snow, rubble, and flowing water with zero domain adaptation. No terrain map. No fine-tuning. ANYmal just does it. Still the clearest proof that sim-to-real for legs is solved. Legs. https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.11251

The mechanism is almost annoyingly simple: randomize terrain in sim, train on joint states and contact history only, never give the network a terrain map to overfit to. That's it. The robustness falls out naturally. Every humanoid dexterity team chasing Dactyl-style hand control should read this…
Honest limit: curated natural terrain is not an active mine face or a flooded nuclear vault under 20kg payload. The gap between 'walks through a stream' and 'inspects a pipe under load' is real. How far do you think proprioceptive controllers scale before exteroception becomes non-negotiable?…

Why does your service robot ignore customers waving at it? Because it only listens. A 6-day live store deployment found 15.3% of interactions started nonverbally. This paper fixes that with cue tokens fed straight into the LLM. https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11633v1

The architecture is actually lean: real-time multi-label video recognizer, cue tokens appended to the LLM prompt, VLM for item recognition. No hand-crafted rules. Runs online. The store environment is genuinely hard, not a controlled lab setup.
Caveat worth flagging: offline eval only covers nonverbal-triggered turns, no live A/B against a baseline audio-only system. We don't have numbers on whether customers actually notice the difference. Anyone seen a deployment study that closes that loop?
