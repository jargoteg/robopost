Dense perception stacks vs. four tiny sensors under the feet. This quadruped navigates stepping stones with zero cameras, zero LiDAR, just proximity sensors in the soles. Task-specific sensing beats general reconstruction here. https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31912

What's clever: the sensor sits at the contact point, so perception and action are spatially co-located. No map, no latency, no occlusion. The policy learns foothold-seeking from that alone. Video: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K7_acxnVrdI
My caveat: stepping stones are geometrically kind. Wet rubble, angled surfaces, or soft ground adds slip and 3D complexity that proximity alone may miss. How far does the 'subtract sensors' argument hold on real field terrain? Anyone tested similar minimal sensing on unstructured outdoor surfaces?

Safe for humans. That tradeoff usually costs the robot. ergoCub co-optimizes hardware AND motion control against human spinal load as a real cost function. Clever framing. One lab platform so far. https://www.nature.com/articles/s42256-026-01272-2

The specific move: spinal load on a nearby worker feeds into the same optimizer that designs limb geometry and posture policy. Not post-hoc. Simultaneous. That coupling is what makes it interesting, not the 'shared intelligence' branding.
Honest question though: how does this hold when the co-worker does something unplanned? Single-platform lab results with one body type tell you the optimization is feasible. They don't tell you it generalizes. What would you need to see before trusting this in a real workspace?
