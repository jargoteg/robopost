BCIs work in the lab. Daily home use is recalibration sessions, training fatigue, and signal drift. IEEE Spectrum asked actual users and the answer is complicated. https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JlO2GU-Bdfc

The field optimizes for decoding accuracy in 20-minute sessions. Users need reliability across a full day, at home, without a technician present. Those are completely different engineering targets and we rarely report on the second one.
Longitudinal in-home usability data is genuinely scarce in BCI research. Until we collect it systematically, we're flying blind on what actually makes a device useful versus abandoned. Anyone know of groups doing rigorous at-home deployment studies right now?

Wheel-legged robots balancing a ball on real grass is a more serious test than it looks. Two coupled problems, outdoor terrain, no lab floor. Full footage: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=T1edFaw-wnA

The interesting part is the coupling. Correcting robot posture shifts the ball. Tracking the ball perturbs posture. Indoors these get decoupled by assumption. Grass takes that assumption away. That's why outdoor demos actually tell you something.
What the video can't tell you: failure rate, how often the ball drops, how performance scales with terrain roughness. A demo is existence proof, not a reliability number. What would you need to see before trusting this outdoors on a real task?

Your orchard robot snaps a branch. MPC saw it coming. Real-time torque sensing + recursive least squares branch stiffness model + MPC replanning = contact forces suppressed, 1mm accuracy held, no joint overload. Clever abstraction. Still… http://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/11593107

The 'virtual contact body' is the part worth stealing. Instead of reacting to force as raw data, they model the branch as a stiffness element and fit it live with RLS. MPC then treats it as a constraint, not a disturbance. That's a cleaner problem formulation than most reactive planners use.
Caveat that matters: every result comes from a simulated harvesting scenario. Real canopies have wind load, variable fruit mass, and branches that snap rather than deform smoothly. Does RLS converge fast enough when contact dynamics change abruptly? That's the field validation nobody has run yet…
